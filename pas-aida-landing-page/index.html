PAS and AIDA, explained with a real landing page.

Two old copywriting formulas show up in every guide, usually as jargon. Here is what PAS and AIDA actually mean, how they fit together, and exactly which landing-page sections each one becomes.

About a 6 minute read.

PAS and AIDA are not competing theories you have to choose between. They are two zoom levels of the same idea: move a reader from "not thinking about this" to "I'll take action," in an order that respects how people actually decide. AIDA is the wide shot of the whole page; PAS is the close-up that makes the top of the page work. Use both.

AIDA: the shape of the whole page

AIDA is Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. Read it as four jobs the page does in sequence:

  • Attention: the hero. Stop the scroll with the outcome the reader wants, in their words. If they cannot tell what they get in five seconds, nothing else matters.
  • Interest: the problem and the "how it works." Show that you understand their situation and that the path out is simple. This is where they decide to keep reading.
  • Desire: features as outcomes, the product shown doing the thing, proof, and pricing. This is where "interesting" becomes "I want this."
  • Action: one clear call to action, repeated at the end. Ask once, with the same words you opened with.

AIDA tells you the order of the page. It does not tell you how to make the top hit hard. That is PAS's job.

PAS: the engine at the top

PAS is Problem, Agitate, Solution. It is the tightest way to create desire before you pitch:

  • Problem: name the reader's situation plainly. "You finished the product, but the landing page is where you stall."
  • Agitate: make the cost of that situation felt, honestly and briefly. "Every week it sits unlaunched is a week of no signups and no feedback."
  • Solution: present your product as the specific way out, and only now.

The mistake almost everyone makes is skipping straight to Solution while the reader still feels fine about their status quo. People buy a solution only after they feel the problem. PAS is the fix.

How they fit together

Picture PAS sitting inside the Attention-Interest part of AIDA. The hero grabs Attention with an outcome. The problem and agitation sections build Interest by making the reader feel the cost. The solution kicks off Desire, which the features, proof, and pricing then deepen. One final ask closes with Action. One page, both frameworks, no conflict.

A worked example, section by section

Here is the combined flow mapped to real sections and a sample line for each, for a fictional standup tool:

Hero (Attention): "Your daily standup, written for you." One outcome, one button.

Problem (Interest): "Status meetings eat an hour a day and still leave people out of the loop."

Agitate (Interest): "Multiply that across the team, every day, and it is the most expensive meeting you run."

Solution (Desire): "Cairn turns your commits and tickets into a clear daily summary, automatically."

Features as outcomes (Desire): "Everyone stays in sync without anyone writing an update."

Proof (Desire): a real quote with a name, then a number.

Pricing (Desire): anchored tiers, one highlighted, a money-back line.

Final CTA (Action): restate the outcome, ask once.

Which one do I reach for?

Use AIDA to lay out the whole page and check nothing is missing or out of order. Use PAS whenever you need a section to persuade in a small space: the hero plus problem block, an ad, a cold email, a single feature. If you only remember one rule, remember the PAS one: name the problem before you sell the solution.
