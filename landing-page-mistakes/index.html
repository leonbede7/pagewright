7 landing page mistakes that quietly kill conversions.

Traffic is arriving and nobody is signing up. Before you blame the ads, check the page. It is almost always one of these seven, and all seven are fixable in an afternoon.

About a 5 minute read.

1. The hero is about you, not the visitor

"We are a next-generation platform" tells the reader nothing they care about. They came to solve their problem, not to learn your company adjectives. Fix: make the headline the outcome the visitor gets, in their words. Lead with what changes for them, then explain how underneath.

2. Two calls to action competing for the same click

"Start free trial" next to an equally loud "Book a demo" splits attention and lowers both. A confused visitor does nothing. Fix: pick one primary action and make it the obvious one. A second option is fine only if it is visibly quieter (a text link, not a second big button).

3. Jumping to the solution before naming the problem

If the page opens by selling features, it skips the step that creates desire: making the reader feel the problem. Fix: add a short section that names the cost of their current situation in concrete terms. People buy a solution only after they feel the problem.

4. Walls of text with no hierarchy

Nobody reads a landing page. They scan it. A dense paragraph is a dead zone. Fix: short headline, one short sub-paragraph, lots of white space. If a section cannot be understood from its headline alone, the headline is doing the wrong job.

5. Social proof that is vague or invented

"Loved by thousands" proves nothing, and a testimonial signed "- Sarah" reads as fake because it usually is. Fix: use one specific, believable quote with a real name, role, and company. If you have no proof yet, leave the section out rather than faking it. An honest gap beats an obvious lie.

6. Pricing with no anchor, or no pricing at all

Hiding the price behind "contact us" loses the self-serve buyer, and a single plan gives the reader nothing to compare against. Fix: show two or three tiers, highlight the one you want chosen, and add a line that removes risk ("no card to start, cancel anytime"). People decide faster when they have an anchor.

7. Asking for too much, too soon

A signup form with nine fields, or a demo request as the only path in, is a wall right when interest is highest. Fix: ask for the minimum, once, with the same words you used in the hero. Every extra field and every extra decision is a place to lose someone.

Notice the pattern: none of these are about how the page looks. They are about order and words. A plain page that gets these right will outconvert a beautiful one that gets them wrong, every time.
