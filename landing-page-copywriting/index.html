Landing page copywriting for developers who hate writing.

You can ship the product. Then you open the landing page, stare at the blank hero, and freeze. Copy is not a talent you are missing. It is a small set of moves you can run every time. Here is the whole framework.

About a 7 minute read.

The good news first: landing page copy is not creative writing. You are not trying to sound clever. You are trying to be clear about one thing, in the reader's own words, in the right order. That is a repeatable process, and a developer who follows it will beat a "real writer" who is just being eloquent.

The one drill that fixes most copy: "so what?"

Almost every weak line on a landing page is a feature stated as a fact. The fix is to ask "so what?" until you reach an outcome the reader actually feels. Keep going until the next "so what?" would sound silly.

Real-time sync.

So what? Everyone sees changes instantly.

So what? Your team is never working off old information.

So what? You stop having the status meeting.

That last line is the copy. It is concrete, it is a result, and it is something the reader wants. The feature ("real-time sync") still belongs on the page, lower down, as the reason the outcome is true. Lead with the outcome, support it with the feature.

The hero: one outcome, one mechanism, one ask

The top of the page has exactly one job: make a stranger understand what they get and why it is believable, in about five seconds. Use three lines and nothing more.

Headline (the outcome): the result the reader walks away with, in their words. Not "the next-generation analytics platform." Try the fill-in-the-blank: "[Audience] [achieves outcome] without [the pain they expect]."

Powerful analytics for modern teams.

Know which features actually get used, without a data team.

Subhead (the mechanism): one sentence on how it works, so the headline is believable. "[Product] does [the specific thing] so you can [the outcome]."

One call to action: a single primary button. A second option is fine only if it is visibly quieter, a text link rather than a competing button. Two equal buttons split attention and lower both.

Name the problem before you pitch (PAS)

People buy a solution only after they feel the problem. The simplest structure for this is PAS: Problem, Agitate, Solution. State the situation the reader is in, make the cost of it concrete, then present your product as the way out.

You do not need to be dramatic. One honest paragraph that says "right now you are doing X, which costs you Y" does the work. The mistake is skipping straight to features while the reader still feels fine about their current setup. If they do not feel the problem, your solution is just noise.

Write every feature as an outcome

List your features in one column. In the next column, run the "so what?" drill on each until it becomes a benefit. Put the benefit on the page as the heading and keep the feature as the supporting line.

Feature: role-based permissions.

Outcome: give clients access without showing them everything.

Feature: exports to CSV and webhook.

Outcome: your data is yours, drop it into whatever you already use.

This single exercise rewrites most of a features section in twenty minutes, and it is the difference between a spec sheet and a page that sells.

Proof, in the buyer's words

One specific, believable quote beats ten vague ones. "Loved by thousands" proves nothing. A line with a real name, role, and a concrete result ("cut our onboarding from a week to a day") proves a lot. If you have no testimonials yet, do not invent them. Use a clear product screenshot or a real number you can stand behind, and leave the rest out. An honest gap beats an obvious fake.

The FAQ is where you handle objections

An FAQ is not for trivia. It is the place to answer the six things quietly stopping someone from buying: price worries, "will this work for my case", switching cost, trust, refunds, and "do I need to be technical". Write each answer in the reader's words, plainly. If the same question keeps coming up over email, it belongs on the page.

One ask, repeated

End the page by restating the transformation in one sentence and asking once, with the same words you used in the hero. Do not introduce a new idea at the finish line. The reader has decided by now; your job is to make the next step obvious, not to add more to think about.

A copy pass you can run in an hour

Put it together as a checklist. Write your headline as an outcome. Add a one-line mechanism. Run "so what?" on every feature. Add a short problem section before the solution. Find one real piece of proof. Answer your six real objections. End with one ask. That is a full landing page of copy, and none of it required talent, only the process.
