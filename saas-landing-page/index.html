SaaS landing page: the sections that convert.

A SaaS landing page has one job: turn a stranger into a trial or a signup. Here is the section order that does that, and what to actually write in each, with examples.

About a 7 minute read.

A SaaS landing page is not a brochure. It has a single, measurable job: get the right visitor to start a trial or create an account. Everything on the page either moves them toward that or it is in the way.

The pages that do this well are not the most beautiful ones. They are the ones with the right sections, in the right order, with copy that talks about the visitor's outcome instead of the product's features. Here is that order, SaaS by SaaS, with example lines you can adapt.

1. Hero: the outcome, the mechanism, one action

A visitor decides in about five seconds whether to keep reading. Your hero has to answer "what do I get" before "what is this." Three elements, no more:

  • Headline = the outcome. Not "AI-powered analytics platform." Try "Know which features actually get used." The first is about you, the second is about them.
  • Subhead = the mechanism, in one line. How the outcome happens. "We track every click and tie it to revenue, so you can cut the features nobody touches."
  • One primary call to action. For SaaS that is almost always "Start free" or "Start your trial." A quiet secondary link ("See how it works") is fine. Two loud buttons is not.

Show the product. A real screenshot or a clean in-app preview beats a stock photo or a gradient blob every time. If the hero is just text on a colored shape, it reads as unfinished.

2. Trust bar: borrow credibility immediately

Right after the hero, doubt arrives. A thin row of customer logos answers it before it grows: "Used by teams at...". No logos yet? Show the tools you integrate with, or a publication that covered you, or leave the row out. Never invent companies.

3. The problem: make the status quo expensive

Before anyone wants your SaaS, they have to feel the cost of how they work now. Name it concretely.

Right now your team ships features on instinct, finds out three months later that half of them are dead weight, and maintains them forever anyway.

Specific pain moves people. "Inefficient workflows" does not. Say the real thing your buyer complains about on a Friday.

4. How it works: three steps to value

SaaS buyers assume setup is a slog, so this section has one job: reduce perceived effort. Three steps, verbs for titles ("Connect", "Track", "Decide"), and a clear "time to first value." If your product takes ten steps to explain, that is an onboarding problem, not a copy problem.

5. Features as outcomes, not a spec sheet

Lead each feature with what it lets the buyer do, then explain the mechanism. The test: finish the sentence "so you can..." and put that first.

  • "Slack and webhook alerts" becomes "Know the moment a key account goes quiet, so you can save the renewal."
  • "SSO and audit logs" becomes "Pass the security review without a three-week back and forth."

For B2B SaaS, two features matter more than buyers admit: integrations (does it fit my stack) and security (will it pass procurement). Give each its due. And avoid the three-identical-cards feature row; vary the layout so the eye does not glaze.

6. Proof: one real voice beats ten generic ones

A testimonial with a name, a role, and a company is worth more than a wall of anonymous praise. Pick the quote that names a specific result. If you have hard numbers you can stand behind ("cut our reporting time from a day to an hour"), use them. Do not invent precise-looking stats; buyers can smell a fake 92.4%.

7. Pricing: anchor it, and show it

Hiding price behind "contact us" loses the self-serve buyer who was ready to start. Show two or three tiers, highlight the one you want chosen (usually the middle), and add the line that removes the last hesitation: "No credit card to start. Cancel anytime." A free or trial tier lowers the cost of saying yes.

8. FAQ: handle the real objections

The FAQ is objection handling in disguise. For SaaS the recurring ones are predictable: "Do you store my data?", "How long does setup take?", "Can I cancel?", "Does it work with [tool]?", "Is there a free plan?". Answer the fear plainly. If you know the top reason people hesitate, make it the first question.

9. Final call to action: ask once, in the same words

Restate the transformation in one line and offer exactly one action, the same one as the hero, with the same label. By now the visitor is convinced or not. Do not introduce new information. Just ask.
